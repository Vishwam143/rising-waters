Project Abstract

Floods are among the most destructive natural disasters worldwide, causing significant loss of life, displacement of communities, and extensive damage to infrastructure. Every year, millions of people are affected due to inadequate early warning systems and delays in flood prediction. Traditional forecasting techniques struggle to provide accurate predictions because of the complex relationships among meteorological parameters.

This project implements a Machine Learning-based Flood Prediction System to predict the likelihood of flood occurrences using historical weather observations. By training multiple supervised models (Decision Trees, Random Forest, KNN, and XGBoost) on high-dimensional weather metrics, our system achieves high-precision early warning flags. The serialized models are coupled with this interactive dashboard, supporting quick scenario analysis and disaster management readiness.

Operational Scenarios

A meteorologist collects current weather observations including annual rainfall, cloud visibility, and seasonal rainfall measurements for a flood-prone district. After entering the data into the application, the XGBoost model predicts a high probability of flooding. Based on this prediction, local authorities issue early evacuation warnings, activate emergency response teams, and prepare relief shelters, significantly reducing the risk to human life.

During the monsoon season, a disaster management coordinator monitors multiple vulnerable regions. By entering weather data for each district, the system generates real-time flood risk predictions. Regions identified as high risk receive immediate attention, allowing authorities to efficiently allocate rescue personnel, medical supplies, food, boats, and emergency equipment where they are needed most.

A government analyst evaluates the performance of the machine learning models using historical flood records and unseen test data. The results indicate that the XGBoost classifier outperforms the other algorithms, achieving an accuracy of 96.55% while maintaining high precision and recall. These findings demonstrate the model's reliability and effectiveness for real-world flood prediction and decision support applications.
